Describe early writing systems and their impact on Mesopotamian cultures.

Probably the most important invention to come out of Sumer was the idea and method of writing. This writing in the beginning was not actual writing, and more like hieroglyphics in the fact that it was based around pictographs. Sumerian writing was originally established as an administrative method, inventorying domestic animals and transactions. These administrative writings, dating back to as early as 3400-3200 BCE were written on clay tablets using a sharp tool called a stylus.

As the years go on and it’s getting around 3000 BCE, the use of Sumerian writing was starting to become more widespread. With this writing being used more and more, that meant that the pictographs were eventually starting to become difficult. They began to take up too much space while conveying the intended message and generally a nuisance when it came to reading and writing. The solution born from this problem was wedge-shaped drawings, called cuneiform. With the invention of cuneiform, writing became easier, and the length of the writing also increased, and thus the infamous Epic of Gilgamesh was written. This new way of writing gave the Mesopotamian people a way to start having records and more order in their daily lives, along with the birth of literature, that would eventually become a very useful tool for the kings and their desire to legitimize themselves publicly.

 

Compare and contrast Assyrian and Persian palace architecture.

Within Assyrian and Persian architecture, the themes and content present commonly overlap. The reliefs found in the palaces of kings Ashurnasirpal and Darius I both depict scenes of imperial subjects carrying goods in a procession and are essentially telling the viewer how to act and what to present to the king, who is a divine figure on earth. The concept of a procession and gift-bearing was not one lost from the time of the Assyrians to the Persians.

Another similarity is the use of animal forms on architecture outside the palace, specifically bulls. Lamassu, human-bull (and sometimes lion) hybrids, are popular guards found outside Assyrian palaces. The Persians include the bull in their column capitals that are also typically flanking the entrance of the palace.

One difference is the adoption of style between these two empires. The Assyrians used their own style and motifs in architecture that became distinctly Assyrian whereas the Persians opted to adopt styles from Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Greece within their architecture.
