The Slipper Slapper

Fig. 1. Aphrodite with Eros and Pan, attributed to the Greek sculptor, ca. 100 BCE, National Archaeological Museum, Greece, inv. Г. 3335. https://www.latsisfoundation.org/content/elib/book_14/nam_en.pdf (accessed September 13, 2023).

             

This sculpture group, made by an unknown Greek sculptor, offers the viewer a very lighthearted scene rather than the more usual serious ones posed by statues. This trio of mythical beings dates to around 100 BCE, about two hundred years after the start of the Hellenistic period. Though the sculpture is a smidge over a couple millennia old, it is still in relatively good condition. Most old statues have several pieces missing or broken off but the only evidence of the passage of time is an arm missing from Eros. This sculpture is carved from Parian marble and stands at about 4.2 feet tall (1.29 meters), making it a little smaller than life-size. It is thought to have been carved in Athens but there is also the possibility that it was created at its origin, the island of Delos. Every part of it has been carved quite smoothly and the sculpture itself has a smooth and soft appearance to it. It was found in 1904 on the island of Delos, in the House of the Poseidoniastai. This sculpture did not travel far and has found its current home in Exhibition Hall 30 of the National Archaeological Museum in Athens, Greece, inventory number Г. 3335. 

The House of the Poseidoniastai was a private club for merchants traveling through the Mediterranean, specifically from Beirut. There is an inscription on the base of the statue: “Διονύσιος Zήνωνος του Θεοδώρου / Bηρύττιος, ευεργέτης, υπέρ εαυτου / καί των τέκνων, θεοις πατρώοις”. This inscription translates to: “Dionysios, son of Zenon and Theodoros, from Beirut, benefactor, dedicated, in favor of himself and of his children, to the ancestral gods”. Though the exact artist of this sculpture group is unknown, there is potential that the person – maybe Dionysios? – who hired the artist is from Beirut, as evidenced by the inscription on the base. Dionysios was probably a merchant who often visited the House of the Poseidoniastai during his travels and decided to have this statue dedicated to bringing some lightheartedness and entertainment to the club. This would have certainly improved the social standing of Dionysios among the club because of his philanthropic addition, earning popularity among anyone who has an appreciation for art and the gods.

The statue clearly depicts the Hellenistic style of Rococo, which is similar to the familiar Baroque, that is the style of many famous sculptures for its immense detail and large movements portrayed within the art. Rococo is different from Baroque in that it offers a much more playful and whimsical image of art rather than something more serious. Rococo often includes satyrs or nymphs, as evidenced by the inclusion of a horned and hooved figure (Pan). The exact name is unclear, the piece is usually referenced by simply just listing the prominent figures shown. With there not being a clear name to the statue, it has acquired nicknames such as the “Slipper Slapper” or “Sandal Slapper”. The Slipper Slapper shows three famous figures from Ancient Greek myth: Aphrodite, Eros, and Pan. Aphrodite is the goddess of love, beauty, and fertility. Eros, the son of Aphrodite and more famously known by his Roman name (Cupid), is essentially the male version of Aphrodite and is the god of love. Eros is also known as a primordial god, but other accounts portray him as the son of Aphrodite, which is the interpretation best suited for the analysis of this sculpture. Pan, the final figure shown, is a satyr who is also the Greek god of the wild, rustic music, and inappropriate behavior; he is fairly like Dionysus regarding what he represents.   

            Though this sculpture is static, the scene depicted is nothing short of dynamic. The sculptor was able to capture a moment of action as shown by the three figures all in a different position and reacting to the others. This group gets its nickname from the action of

Aphrodite, who is attempting to fend off Pan with the threat of hitting him with her shoe. Upon looking closer, there seems to be a sandal on one foot, and the “missing” sandal appears to be the one in Aphrodite’s hand, raised and ready to smack Pan with. Aphrodite’s hair is pulled back into an up-do, and she is, unsurprisingly of Greek art, naked. Her smoothly sculpted body is modeled after the average female body of that time and does not give an unrealistic view on how it should look.  One thing to note though is that the display of a naked female body was not entirely common until the 4th century BCE, until that point much of the nudity seen was reserved for heroic men. The nude image was meant to invoke a meaning of power, so this most likely preliminary presentation of a naked woman, Aphrodite, could have caught viewers somewhat off guard and drew their attention even more. With the free hand not poised to strike, Aphrodite is covering her genitalia in response to Pan and his advances. 

            Pan is depicted here acting in a rather intrusive fashion. He is coming up from behind Aphrodite and it appears that he is making a sexual advance on her, based on the covering of genitalia. Pan is shown as fairly muscular in his human torso and detailed in his animalistic legs. The most detail is on his thighs, where grouped swooping patterns form the illusion of swirls of fur. His feet are hooved and it seems there is an extra carved section of the sculpture that acts as a stabilizing beam for Pan. He is also shown to have a curly beard and hair that is a little bit unruly. The presence of horns along with the disorderly grooming all furthermore suggest to the viewer that this man is indeed Pan because of the wild traits seen and the similarity to barbarianism. Due to the not quite fully human aspect of Pan, his portion of the sculpture is not quite realistic in what a man should look like. Despite that, the combination of human and animal aspects are blended quite well together in this piece and offer a convincing idea of how exactly a hybrid of these qualities would look. Pan has both of his hands on Aphrodite, one around her back and the other on the arm covering her front. The congregation of hands around Aphrodite’s private area leads the eye there and to how this interaction implies an advance by Pan on the goddess. His attempt to remove Aphrodite’s hand emphasizes the sexual nature of this interaction and how it is unwarranted by Aphrodite. 

            Eros, the third figure, is what helps to tie together the elements of Aphrodite and Pan to help the viewer understand the image and what it is attempting to show. Eros is seen aiding Aphrodite as he is grabbing Pan by one of the horns on his head. Compared to the other two figures, Eros is a lot smaller in size. He also has wings and is flying as he grabs

Pan’s horn. Due to his small size, there is no way that Eros would ever succeed in fending off Pan fully on his own but that doesn’t seem like the main point. Eros in this sculpture helps to add to the playful setting that is conveyed, if it were just between Aphrodite and Eros, then the scene might be more serious. Eros being there is laughable when the viewer thinks about how Pan tried to make this advance in the presence of at least one other person. The presence of Eros helps the audience realize the woman is Aphrodite and vice versa, adding more context for the intended audience to draw a narrative from. 

            This example of Hellenistic Rococo was meant to be viewed by merchants traveling the Mediterranean, who in ancient times were pretty much all men. Based on who is present and the inscription, the viewer is expected to have knowledge of Greek myth and religion in order to be able to fully take in what the sculptor was trying to portray. The mostly male audience of this piece of art will have a different view on it than if the audience were mostly female. Both parties will view this as a playful piece of art, but women could also see a different meaning that men might not even consider. This piece centers around sexual advances and the rejection of said actions in a mischievous manner.

The humorousness on the subject can lead to questions on the idea of consent, which is an issue even to this day. It can give off the impression that sexual advances and consent are not to be taken entirely seriously, giving the word “no” a less significant meaning. Pan’s action of physically going after Aphrodite and putting his hands on her further exemplifies how men do not care to take women and their agency seriously. Men who view this sculpture will most likely not take the time to take these things into consideration but rather in a humorous and boyish way, especially for the period they live in. The ideas behind consent and inappropriate persistence are not fully formed in the minds of men and they will simply view this sculpture as its superficial value and story.
