Comparison between black and red figure painting

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Black-figure painting was great and all but gave artists limitations bc of the fact that it’s harder to be versatile

            -red figure painting was the solution to this problem, invented by the Andokides painter

Red-figure painting was the complete opposite and used positive space (red) to stand out from negative space (black)

            -painting this way allowed for artists to no longer have to carve the details from the black, but rather draw the details on the red -> greater flexibility

            -this technique helped revolutionize drawing

PARTHENON

The main part of the Athenian Acropolis (acropolis meaning a city upon a hill) was the Parthenon, dedicated to Athena Parthenos

            -Parthenon had no priest or alter so technically, not a temple

A lot of the building in this complex took several years and continued after the death of Pericles

            -made of the finest marble

Architect of Parthenon was Iktinos who was thought to be assisted by Kallikrates

            -giant statue inside was done by Phidias, who is said by Plutarch to have been in charge of the entire Acropolis project

            -Athena Parthenos statue was chryselephantine (made of gold and ivory), thought that Iktinos designed the Parthenon around her

 

 

Parthenon is built to mathematical precision

            -irregular though because it is a Doric style building with Ionic elements

                        >back of temple has Ionic columns and inner frieze of the temple is also Ionic

The metopes of the Parthenon display common themes such as the gigantomachy, amazonomachy, and centauromachy

Ionic frieze inside Parthenon is not entirely clear on subject matter but it is thought to depict that Panathenaic Festival procession

            -this is noteworthy bc humans had never been depicted on a temple before

ERECHTHEION

Construction began in 421 BCE after the Persians had destroyed the Archaic Athena temple

Building site for this was on uneven ground, so each side of the building is different

Discussion questions

Ancient Greek architecture became a dominant influence in Western civilization. What forms and functions of buildings across periods of Greek history are notable? How did the orders of Greek architecture define these styles?

Greek architecture has been continuously reused time and time again thanks to it being fairly easy to point out. Probably the most famous aspects of Greek architecture are the three column orders, which then dictates how a temple is built in order to match that column motif. The three big ones are Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian. Each of these gets more ornate than the last, with increased amounts of bases and intricate swirling designs. Columns served a functional and aesthetic purpose, but over time came to be infamous for their aesthetic.

A building function we borrow from the Greeks is the theater, which was founded in Greece. Originally, theaters just consisted of patches of earth where people would act. As time went on, these “theaters” began to include seating, often scaling up a hill, in order to give viewers an unobstructed view of the show. This general layout has stayed quite consistent over the years, with theaters and any other kind of large viewing venue following this almost cone shape with the seating and stage.

 

Representations of humans in ancient Greek sculpture evolved from static, conventionalized forms to idealized forms to more expressionistic figures. How can we see this progression in examples from different periods? What sculpting techniques affected these representations?

This progression of static to dynamic figures in Greek art can be seen quite clearly when looking at all of the time periods as a whole, but the steps taken between each period is a lot smaller. The representations of humans in Greek art initially is quite rigid and static, following canon that is very similar to the Egyptians. The rigidity of sculpture during this earlier period is also due to the medium used. During this time, the marble used was typically lesser in value than the marble we are used to seeing today, so it was quite difficult to carve and didn’t look the best.

Over time, however, the Greeks and their artists begin to have a form of individuality that they did not have before, resulting in some more variation in works produced. This was allowed for by the use of different kinds of marble, such as Pentelic or Naxian, which were much higher in quality. The Lady of Auxerre is a good example of Greek art that is still rigid in nature but is beginning to turn toward that more naturalistic side. This progression can be seen through pieces such as the Kritios Boy and Riace Bronzes as they start to have a much more curvy shape to their body. This goes on to the extreme during the Hellenistic period, where pieces were made with extreme detail and emotion that made them feel life-like.
