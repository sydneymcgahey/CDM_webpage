Alexander and Bucephalus

 

Fig. 1. Statuette of Alexander the Great on Horseback, attributed to the Roman artist, ca. 1st century BCE, National Archaeological Museum, Naples, Italy.

https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/629492 (accessed October 10, 2023).

 

 

 

            This statuette is unsurprisingly a Roman copy of an earlier artwork and was crafted by

Antonio Reder around the first century BCE. It was excavated from the Theater of

Herculaneum in 1761 in Naples, Italy. After a few owners, this statuette found its permanent home in the National Archaeological Museum of Naples, however, it is currently on loan to the MET for an exhibition on Greek and Roman art. The original statue that this copy is molded after is a bronze group that Alexander the Great had commissioned one of his favorite artists, Lysippos, to create (Siano). This is a miniature version of that statue by Lysippos, measuring about 19.2 inches by 18.5 inches. 

            The original statue stood amongst a group as a monument at Dion on the slopes of Mount Olympus in Greece. The main reason Alexander had this statue commissioned was to commemorate the battle at the Granicus (also spelled as Granikos) River in 334 BCE in Turkey against the Achaemenid Persians. The commission by Alexander the Great along with the immense detail of the statue means this is about as Hellenistic as it gets. The battle this commemorates was in 334 BCE and Alexander the Great died in 323 BCE, so the original had to have been commissioned sometime in that eleven year period. 

This piece is made from bronze and silver and instead of being one piece, it is comprised of four parts. The rider, horse, base, and rudder are all separate and held together with screws and bolts. With the statuette being made piecewise, it means that a lot of thought was put into the design. The pieces would have to be able to fit together once they were cast and screwed to each other, taking the different textures into place, like the horse’s mane and Alexander’s skirt (Siano). This copy of Lysippos’s work is renowned for its attention to detail, especially the armor of Alexander and body of Bucephalus. Bucephalus’s muscles are captured in a realistic way that makes it look as if there is no rudder supporting him at all and he is readying to charge forward.

Alexander is seen astride his beloved horse, Bucephalus (also spelled as

Boukephalas). The whole statue is dynamic as both Alexander and his horse are caught in a pose that might be showing a moment in a battle. This is typical of depictions of Alexander, as he carefully chooses how to represent himself and only chooses scenes from battles, hunts, processions, marriages, and pageants. All of these scenes are not new to art and have commonly been employed to display kings and their kingship, so of course Alexander had to utilize this visual tactic. Bucephalus is reared back on his hind legs with his front legs somewhat splayed out and his expression features an open mouth and wide eyes, seeming like he is getting ready to jump into battle. Alexander is also in a similar pose of readiness as evidenced by his turn to potentially face someone and the armor he is wearing. There is also a scabbard seen on him and his raised right arm indicates that he was brandishing his sword, ready to strike an enemy. 

Upon original arrival to its destination, this Roman copy was broken in places and some arms and legs were missing. These pieces were quickly restored by the Royal Palace of Portici in Naples in 1762 (Siano). The base supporting Bucephalus was not added until much later in the 18th century. This base supposedly represents the Granicus River, which could be further be interpreted as Alexander atop Bucephalus to protect this river and fight off invading armies from it. If the base does represent the river, the literal support it gives Bucephalus could also be figurative as well, implying that the river – and therefore the gods – support Alexander and his fight against the Persians. 

            Alexander the Great is portrayed in his typical fashion: clean shaven, tousled hair, and anastole. In case if the viewer was unsure of who was atop the horse leading men into battle, Alexander had to make sure to include his most defining characteristics when it comes to physical appearances in art. This monument was likely also used to serve as a means for propaganda for Alexander. In the Battle of Granicus, the first charge failed and only during the second one was Alexander the Great successful. Neither Lysippos nor Alexander would be inclined to bring that up so that fact is omitted, making the statue represent the initial first charge into battle by a great leader. Despite facing the terror of charging first into battle, Alexander is shown to have a calm expression. This is indicative of the confidence and skills that he has and shows the people looking at this statue that they have nothing to fear. If Alexander can charge into battle without a trace of fear on his face, then the people under him know that he will protect them will bravery and come out victorious. It serves to instill a belief in the people for Alexander and his place as their ruler.

This would have also served to remind the viewers of Alexander’s prowess and skills as a military leader. It would there become just another way Alexander legitimizes his reign and skills amongst the people of his empire. His use of propaganda was popular and important when it came to his reign because of the vast size of his empire. It rivaled other great empires, but Alexander was only one person, and it was difficult to enact the same amount of control across different cultures and people. Monuments like this showing Alexander in heroic moments and exemplifying his excellence help tighten his grip a little bit. Another way this statue raises Alexander on a pedestal is the fact that he is on his horse, it brings him up to a higher level both figuratively and literally. 

This monument is present on Mount Olympus, which is the famed place of the gods. The placement of this monument of this infamous mountain could not have been done by coincidence. Alexander is very purposeful and deliberate when it comes to his benefactions and commissions, as if art itself required the same amount of strategy as war. His position upon the mountain close to the gods shows his divinity and relationship he has with the gods, who will back him and his continuing claim for legitimization.
