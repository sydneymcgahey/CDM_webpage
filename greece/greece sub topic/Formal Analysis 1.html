Marble Statue Group of the Three Graces

s

Fig. 1. Marble Statue Group of the Three Graces, attributed to the Roman sculptor, ca. 2nd century CE, The Metropolitan Museum of Art, acc. 2010.260. https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/256403 (accessed September 11, 2023)

 

The carved white marble statue dating back to the second century CE during the Roman imperial period, depicts three young women standing together in a dance-like pose. This is a copy of an earlier Greek work, and its dimensions are about four by three feet. This piece of artwork was originally found in Rome in 1892, near the Forum of Nerva and Vespasian’s Temple of Peace. Over the years it has bounced around between owners but has been housed at the MET since 2010.  

The three Graces, also known as the Charities, were three daughters of Zeus who could bestow gifts upon humanity. Gifts they were thought to hold were fertility, growth, beauty in the arts, and peaceful reciprocity between men. They were named Thalia, Algaia, and Euphrosyne, each representing abundance, beauty, and mirth, respectively. The Graces played a role in festivals and loved to organize dancing but most notably were the personal handmaidens of Aphrodite, goddess of love, beauty, and fertility.

Each girl is standing shoulder to shoulder in a straight line and alternating in which ways they face. They are not standing completely upright, each one has a popped hip and bent knee, each deliberately unevenly shifting their weight. This method of standing in sculpture first started to appear during the Classical period starting in 480 BCE and continued to be a staple into the imperial Roman period several hundred years later. This pose creates more depth in the sculpture as there are now more curved lines to the body. The new angles and lines give the eye more to look at than a figure standing rigid. The girls each have one arm draped over the one adjacent to them in a loose fashion, elbow hanging over the chest or upper back. The other arm on each figure that is supposed to be hanging at the side is missing, alongside every head and neck.

Flanking each end of the sculpture are some kind of amphorae, both of which have a cloth draped over the top. Given that all three of the women are naked, it is possible that the cloth covering the amphorae are their garments. The amphorae themselves are plain and show no ornamentation. Since the amphorae are not decorated or in the center, they are obviously not meant to be the focal point but were intentionally carved, nonetheless. The combination of amphorae and women in a structured formation could indicate this is a scene from where the Graces could be found: dancing at a festival.

            Arms, heads, and necks are commonly, almost notoriously, missing from ancient statues and sculptures. The reason is very simple, and it is this: those places are thinner and break off easier. As the Graces are handmaidens of Aphrodite, the missing heads must be beautiful. The Graces’ bodies in the sculpture show that of young, healthy women with no sort of blemish or scar anywhere to be found. The combination of youth and health of the women symbolizes their relationship with divinity.

            In antiquity when this statue was standing at its prime (having arms and heads), the effect of it had to have been profound on the viewer. The Graces positioned together in such a way create this sense of ease and comfort that the viewer doesn’t want to look away from. The draping of the arms around each other and movement of the hips is such a subtle change when compared to rigid figures, but the capture of movement invites the viewer to want to feel that movement themselves. The viewer gets to feel almost divine as they get to witness the three Graces dance. The handmaidens to Aphrodite are now acting as bridge between two worlds, bringing them together: the realm of the gods and the realm of men. This inclusion of the viewer can make them feel closer to the gods.

            Given that the inspiration for this replica is from ancient Greek times, the feeling of dancing with higher beings or even more of a sense of closeness could most likely have been the intended meaning. Being found in an area near a forum and temple of peace, this sculpture was most likely meant to be seen by the public who walked through that area. This statue does not seem to be that of propaganda. Instead of spreading kingly belief and authority, the three Graces fill out a space where different people converged and offer a positive and enlightening message.  Greece didn’t have kings or emperors that needed to communicate their messages and authority, they answered to the gods. This sculpture does not need to be heavily symbolic or ornate because it is not a product of imperialism, not technically. It was made during an imperial time by a subject of imperialism but the item itself lends itself only to older Greek beliefs.
