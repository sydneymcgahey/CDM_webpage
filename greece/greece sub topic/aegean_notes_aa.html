Aegean – Bronze Age

Early Cycladic art (3000-2000 BCE):

This region is known for its marble and dragon glass (obsidian)

            -very difficult to carve marble at this time

Cycladic  art of the Cyclades Islands (named bc they “circle” around Delos) and other islands in the Aegean not including Crete

            -Cycladic statues often depict naked women

Marble was abundant in quarries across the Aegean islands, especially on Naxos and Paros (Naxian and Parian marble)

**these figurines are the start of the marble sculpture tradition in Greece

Middle and Late Minoan art (1700-1200 BCE):

This time period is when Greece starts to interact w/ other people around the Mediterranean

Minoan  art of Crete

During this time in Crete and on the mainland, people typically weren’t buried w/ goods

            -architecture and palaces are more common from this period

This era is when the first great Western civilization emerged

Many of the “palaces” built did not serve as royal housing

            -used for administration, commercial, ceremonies, etc

The main “palaces” on Crete are Knossos, Phaistos, Malia, Kato Zakro, and Khania

            -show power and prosperity of Minoan

The largest palace on Crete was Knossos and legendary home of King Minos

-complicated and intricate floorplan of the palace led to myths of the labyrinth and eventually the Minotaur

Discussion questions

Apply the vocabulary and concepts used to discuss Bronze Age Aegean art, artists, and art history.

Bronze Age art and architecture experienced a lot of new techniques, especially within the use of metals and stone structures. The infamous “Mask of Agammemnon”  is important not because it is incorrectly named, but because of the item itself. This funerary mask was made using the repousse technique, meaning the gold is hammered out into a thin piece then the details are pushed out from behind. This mask is also the first life-size sculpted image of a face.

The Treasury of Atreus is another good example of numerous Aegean concepts. The Treasury is actually a type of tomb, called a tholos tomb, “tholos” simply meaning that this structure has a burial chamber within it. The hallway-like entrance to the tholos, similarly called a dromos, leads the visitor through a corbeled arch into the burial chamber, which is constructed of the same corbeled arch. Corbeled arches consist of a few components such as two monoliths topped by a lintel to form the gateway and a triangular area above it called a relieving triangle. This relieving triangle helps to lessen the burden of the heavy stone over the archway so that it doesn’t collapse.

 

Interpret ancient Aegean art using appropriate art historical methods, such as observation, comparison, and inductive reasoning.

Aegean art from the Early Cycladic period to the later Minoan and Mycenaean periods undergoes a change that starts to bring more detail into art and architecture. The Cycladic period is dominated by forms that are abstract and more ambiguous in meaning. Going into the time of the Minoans and Myceneans, art and architecture begins to change as they finally begin to interact with other people around the Mediterranean.

Minoan and Mycenean art is best represented through frescoes found within palaces. The subject differs between two groups of people, Minoans commonly depicted nature whereas the Myceneans focused more on war in their art. Their art was extremely sought after, specifically motifs such as the flying gallop commonly seen in frescoes. This principle of composition is seen in art all around the Mediterranean once the Minoans begin utilizing it, evidencing the connectivity that Aegean people are having with others and the spread of their artistic influence.
