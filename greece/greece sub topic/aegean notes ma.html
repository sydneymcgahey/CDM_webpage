Middle and Late Minoan art (1700-1200 BCE):

Minoan  art of Crete

Divided into Old Palace Period and New Palace Period

            -this division was created by an earthquake that destroyed a lot of architecture

During this time in Crete and on the mainland, people typically weren’t buried w/ goods

            -architecture and palaces are more common from this period

This era is when the first great Western civilization emerged

Many of the “palaces” built did not serve as royal housing and Crete was probably run by aristocrats

            -used for administration, commercial, ceremonies, etc

The main “palaces” on Crete are Knossos, Phaistos, Malia, Kato Zakro, and Khania

            -show power and prosperity of Minoans

 

The largest palace on Crete was Knossos and legendary home of King Minos, built after earthquake

            -complicated and intricate floorplan of the palace led to myths of the labyrinth and eventually the Minotaur

Considered to be the highest point of Minoan civilization

Discussion questions

Cycladic sculptors followed strict conventions in their depiction of human figures. Describe characteristics of these sculptures, the methods used to produce them, and interpretations of their function or value to their culture according to art historians.

The strict conventions of Cycladic figurines make them quite easy to point out and differentiate. These figurines follow abstract yet realistic conventions in their portrayal of the human body. They are slightly similar to Egyptian style in how the angle and shapes of the body are cut, there is not a lot of detail on the figurines, but each feature of the body can easily be visualized from the abstract form. These figurines were predominantly women during the earlier parts of the Cycladic period and commonly found in graves.

They were positioned with their arms folded over the stomach and feet pointed, so they are unable to stand upright. The combination of signs of fertility (arms folded over stomach) with being in a grave is not entirely clear as far as the symbolism, though one interpretation could be that this is related to the afterlife and rebirth. One issue with these figurines is how popular and easy to make they are. They are made of marble specifically from the Cycladic islands Naxos and Paros with specific carving tools. As long as someone has those materials, these figurines can be reproduced.

How did fresco techniques and applications evolve in Aegean cultures? Distinguish between two types of fresco introduced in the chapter. What subjects or types of imagery are evident in surviving wall paintings?

Fresco was quite popular in the Aegean and even influenced art around the Mediterranean. The Minoans pioneered the buon fresco or simply just fresco technique, where the painting is done while the plaster is still wet. This required speed and precision on the artist because plaster dried quite quickly. This type of fresco is much more durable than the other type, fresco secco, as it allows the paint to be combined with the wall. Fresco secco, on the other hand, is the opposite of buon fresco and includes painting after the plater has already dried. This type of fresco is more brittle because the paint can chip off quite easily, but the pigment is much more vivid since it is on top and not with the plaster.

The best-preserved frescoes come from the Minoan civilization on Crete, which often depict scenes of nature and wildlife. A famous fresco from the palace of Knossos is the Bull Leaping Fresco. This piece has the typical flying gallop composition that is distinctively Aegean and shows a Cretan tradition of jumping bulls. Another great example of Aegean fresco is from the Minoan-inspired Akrotiri, this fresco shows scenes of women picking crocuses and spans multiple levels. Like in other frescoes around the palace and other parts of the Aegean, the scenes depicted often show the interaction of humans with nature and wildlife in a way that is representational of everyday life.
