Figurine Group of Musicians

 

Fig. 1. Group of clay female figurines, attributed to the Greek artist, ca. 250 – 225 BCE, Louvre,

Paris, inv. CA 799. https://collections.louvre.fr/en/ark:/53355/cl010263966 (accessed September 26, 2023).

 

This group of small figurines has no name but still manages to tell a tale of merriment as the women play instruments. Estimated to have been made sometime between 250 and 225 BCE, these figures were found on Aegina, an island off the coast of Athens. Details beyond the date and general location of their excavation are not provided, so the guess on where these figurines would have exactly been housed during their time could be extrapolated at best. On Aegina there are two temples that had been standing during the time of the figurines’ existence, one each dedicated to Apollo and Athena Aphaia. Aphaia was a goddess who was the daughter of Zeus and Karme. The Hellenistic period is when there starts to be a decline in the dedication to polytheism, however, temples and traditional ritual practices were still popular in Greek society. This idea was most pronounced in the art produced during this period, which gave the modern world some of the most infamous sculptures of deities. Since there are two temples on the small island of Aegina and not much else, there is a fair chance that these figurines could have originally been from one of them.  

Another thing to note since the details of their finding location are not recorded: there is the possibility that there are missing figurines from this set, which could potentially change the scene and how it is interpreted. After an unknown history of these figurines post original finding, they managed to land themselves into the Department of Greek, Etruscan, and Roman Antiquities in the Louvre in 1897 from the owner, Galanis. The group is currently available for viewing at the Louvre in the Sully Wing on level one, room 646, inventory number CA 799. Each one is nearly ten inches tall and is carefully molded from reddish-brown molded clay that has then been finely painted. Every piece also has a vent hole somewhere on it for when it is fired. Other than a few missing fingers and some of the figurines being pieces glued back together, this set is in quite good condition and the details of the figures are preserved well. 

Of the five figurines, three are standing while two are seated on small benches. The sitting figures are each accompanied by a smaller figure. The small figures do not have wings and only one is depicted as nude so they cannot be erotes. The women that are sitting with the smaller figures are looking at them as they play, as if they are somehow involved in the situation. This look could be one of approval but could also be one of closeness, as if the musician is trying to make a connection with the person sitting with them. Perhaps they simply are children that are sitting with the women and listening to them play.

Four figurines are holding string instruments and playing them while a fifth one, which is standing, is not holding a string instrument but her arms are raised as she is in fact playing something else. All the instruments being played look like some sort of lyre or kithara, except for the fifth one that is holding two smaller cylinders. It is hard to tell exactly what she is holding but it could be a sistrum in each hand, a traditionally Egyptian instrument that is a type of rattle. This instrument is commonly used during religious ceremonies. The kithara was typically used by professional musicians and played during competitions or performances. 

Each figurine is dressed in clothing that has been delicately draped over their bodies and the smooth ripples in the clay make the fabric come to life. This illusion of real fabric is done using a technique that started in the High Classical period, called “wet drapery.” They also all have their hair pulled back into some sort of style, some seem to have a sort of band or flower-looking crown adorning their hair. The way their curled hair is pulled back into buns is done in a style called melon ribs, to make the hair look more voluminous and therefore more life-like. Two women even are shown to have feathered wings which gives an angelic and divine aura. Other than two of the figurines having wings, each woman is a realistic portrayal of the typical Greek woman, her looks and form are not idealized. The skin of each figure has been painted a pink color and their hair was painted with a red ochre. The paint has since faded away, but the traces were still able to be identified. Every figurine is delicately carved and full of immense detail as each one is positioned differently. The women all appear to be in the middle of dancing and playing music, the seated ones perhaps singing. 

This group of figurines was most likely housed in the Temple of Apollo or Temple of Athena Aphaia as dedications to the gods, also known as anathēmata. Apollo, who is a god of many things but music being included among those, is likely to have been the one who the figurines were made for. The combination of human and potentially divine figures represents a close relationship with the gods, or just god, in this case. This close relationship would only stem if there was a similarity between them and the divine, in this case it would be music. These figures in their temple would have perhaps been used among items of worship, their level of detail and presence of some winged women bring up the status of the art to that of something more divine rather than mundane. 

Since these pieces are small and easily handled, they could have been arranged among other offerings in a variety of scenes that all still show reverence to the gods. Though the symbolism would remain the same, this offers a more personal approach to worship. Picturing these figurines among other devotions on an altar within a temple, possibly in front of a statue, they invoke the feeling of being at a festival and celebrating via their curved bodies and instruments in hand. This celebration would most certainly be for the gods, who the Greek society still has not forgotten about and continues to dedicate art to.
