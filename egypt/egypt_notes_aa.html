Ancient Egypt

Resources in Egypt: ivory, gold, glass, copper, ebony

Egyptian gods:

Amen aka Re: creator god who is later associated with the sun, created other gods of Egypt

Shu and Tefnut: male and female forces in the universe, born from Re

Geb: earth, progeny of Shu and Tefnut

Nut: sky, progeny of Shu and Tefnut

Osiris: god of order, thought to have brought civilization to Nile valley > after resurrection, becomes lord of the Underworld

Seth: evil opposite of Osiris, god of Chaos, murdered Osiris and cut him into pieces which he spread across Egypt

Isis: sister and consort to Seth, collected Osiris’s body parts and brought him back w/ her magic

Horus: son of Osiris, avenged his father and displaced Seth as King of Egypt > makes Osiris lord of underworld

            -has either falcon head or appears solely as falcon

Khonsu: son of Re and Mut (Re’s consort), moon god

Thoth: moon god, also god of knowledge and writing, associated w/ rebirth and afterlife, watches the judgement of the feather and the heart

            -ibis or baboon

Hathor: divine mother of all Egypt kings, aids dead in their passage to the other world

            -cow headed or woman with cow’s horns

Anubis: god of the afterlife and in charge of guiding souls, god of embalming

            -jackal head

Maat: goddess of truth and right doing, her ostrich feather is what the heart is weighed against on the scale of judgement

Ammit: crocodile head, front body of lion, back of hippo, eats heart/soul if heavier than feather

Discussion questions

Describe the role of the Nile River in Egyptian life as well as Egyptian art and architecture.

The Nile is a huge reason why Egypt became a powerful empire full of resources for so long. This river spanned much of the Egyptian territory and flooded annually, providing fertile land for agriculture and a thriving civilization. Even their creation myth is inspired by the flooding of the Nile, stating that Amen emerged from a muddy mound around limitless water.

The fording of the Nile was an event that could be seen in art surrounding tombs. Often reliefs would show people, animals, and goods crossing the river which was symbolic of passing into the afterlife.

Following the collapse of the Middle Kingdom, the New Kingdom found its source of power in Thebes, along the Nile. This proximity to the life-giving river allowed them to be able to build magnificent temples along the banks, such as the Deir el-Bahri

 

Outline the basic tenets and divinities of the ancient Egyptian religion, especially as regards the afterlife.

Ancient Egypt was famously polytheistic, with gods having roles in every aspect of life. Upon mummification of the body and the attempt for an everlasting ka, Egyptians believed that the heart of the deceased was then weighed on the scales of judgement against the ostrich feather of Maat, goddess of truth and right doing. This judgement is watched by Ammit, who devours your heart if it heavier than the feather, and Anubis, who is the god of the afterlife and in charge of guiding souls to judgement.

Other Egyptian deities who specifically are involved with the afterlife include Osiris, who is the lord of the Underworld following his resurrection, Hathor, and Thoth. Hathor, mother of all Egyptian kings, guides the dead into the underworld while Thoth, god of truth and knowledge (also associated with afterlife and rebirth) joins Ammit in watching the heart’s judgement.
