Ancient Egypt

Resources in Egypt: ivory, gold, glass, copper, ebony

Amen aka Re: creator god who is later associated with the sun, created other gods of Egypt

Shu and Tefnut: male and female forces in the universe, born from Re

Geb: earth, progeny of Shu and Tefnut

Nut: sky, progeny of Shu and Tefnut

Osiris: god of order, thought to have brought civilization to Nile valley > after resurrection, becomes lord of the Underworld

Seth: evil opposite of Osiris, god of Chaos, murdered Osiris and cut him into pieces which he spread across Egypt

Isis: sister and consort to Seth, collected Osiris’s body parts and brought him back w/ her magic

Horus: son of Osiris, avenged his father and displaced Seth as King of Egypt > makes Osiris lord of underworld

            -has either falcon head or appears solely as falcon

Khonsu: son of Re and Mut (Re’s consort), moon god

Thoth: moon god, also god of knowledge and writing, associated w/ rebirth and afterlife, watches the judgement of the feather and the heart

            -ibis or baboon

Hathor: divine mother of all Egypt kings, aids dead in their passage to the other world

            -cow headed or woman with cow’s horns

Anubis: god of the afterlife and in charge of guiding souls, god of embalming

            -jackal head

Maat: goddess of truth and right doing, her ostrich feather is what the heart is weighed against on the scale of judgement

Ammit: crocodile head, front body of lion, back of hippo, eats heart/soul if heavier than feather

 

Pre-dynastic and early dynastic: 3500 – 2575 BCE

Modern Egyptology dates back to 18th century

During early dynastic, Egypt’s kings were seen as gods on earth in human form

            -30 years into king’s reign was a heb sed festival, rekindled king’s divinity

Rosetta stone > Demotic (Egyptian written dialect), hieroglyphs, and Greek

Egypt was divided geographically and politically into Upper (southern Nile) and Lower (northern Nile) Egypt

            -major predynastic art comes from Upper Egypt

Predynastic period ended with unification of Upper and Lower Egypt, done by King Narmer

            -note different headdresses worn by Narmer, wearing the White Crown on one side and the Red on the other

Two cow heads with woman’s face  represents Hathor: divine mother of all Egyptian kings

            -between cow heads is Narmer’s name in hieroglyphics

Slaying motif = became standard pictorial to signify inevitable triumph of Egyptian god-kings over enemies

King appears in the middle in his human form and again on same side as a falcon (“Living Horus”)

-falcon has one human arm, captive human-headed hieroglyph w/ papyrus plants to symbolize victory over Lower Egypt

Ankh and scarab: eternal life

< Mastabas: standard tomb type from early Egypt

Originally housed single burials but later would include several individuals

Imhotep: first recorded name of any artist in the world, master builder for King Djoser during 2600s BCE, also served as high priest of Re

            -after his death, was deified as the sun god Ptah

Imhotep constructed a stepped pyramid (the first) for Djoser at Saqqara, Memphis’s necropolis

            -first grand Egyptian tomb, composed of a series of Mastabas, resembles ziggurat

            -inside had vast network of rooms, meant to be palace in afterlife

Initially, pyramids were quite large and imposing, but decreased in size over time

            -Egypt is just in general less powerful in the future than they were during this time so they just didn’t have the power

Sculptures were often placed in tombs for the ka (soul) if the physical body was damaged

 

< diorite sculpture of Khafre

 

Often left offerings to the dead so they could live on in the afterlife

            -ka lives on in the corpse, hence why the bodies are mummified so that they don’t get damaged

            -also why ka statues are a thing, are there if the body is damaged and just as like a ka safeguard

 

 

 

Old Kingdom: 2575 – 2134 BCE

Time of social and political stability

First of the three great Egyptian periods, rulers amassed great wealth and used it for architectural projects, most notably the pyramids at Gizeh

            -pyramid = symbol of the sun

Built over 75 years, pyramids were tombs for Fourth Dynasty kings Khufu, Khafre, and Menkaure (heir to Khafre) > largest one for Khufu

            -each side of each pyramid is oriented to cardinal points

The Great Sphinx, carved from the Gizeh quarry, most likely represents Khufu

            -largest statue in Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Persia

-wearing the nemes (the striped pharaoh headdress) but uraeus is missing (stylized Egyptian upright cobra, symbol for kingship and goddess Wadjet)

 

 

< relief from the Tomb of Ti (Fifth Dynasty official)

Hunting scenes: symbolic for overcoming evil

Ti is twice the size of the rest of the men and is aloof, symbolizes his away-ness and journey from the living to the dead

Other narrative reliefs in the tomb depict farming/hunting scenes

            **individual’s afterlife is reflection of life on earth

 

 

 

 

 

Though most of Egyptian art includes that of pharaohs and people with power, there were still representations of less prominent people

 

< Seated Scribe, discovered near tomb of gov’t official

Non-royal people where often depicted in a more realistic fashion and much less idealized

Slight sag in his stomach shows the sedentary lifestyle he lived via his profession

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Middle Kingdom: 2040 – 1640 BCE

Sculptors mostly kept to Old Kingdom tradition but there were still advancements

Collapse of Old Kingdom was followed by ~150 years of political unrest

            -first half of Middle Kingdom was stable but by the second half, it started to fall apart

< head of Senusret III, yellow quartzite

Contains idealized body, realistic features of the king

            -new to Egypt in this period!!

            -Middle Kingdom chose to represent the hardship and fragility of the human existence

Period of chaos and political instability, time where lots of foreign leaders were coming in

Also during the Middle Period is when rock-cut tombs became popular

            -entrance was a portico (projecting porch)

 

New Kingdom: 1550 – 1070 BCE

Most glorious period during Egyptian history, when Egyptian kings became known as pharaohs

            -Thutmose III (Hatshepsut his regent) was the first ruler to call himself “pharaoh” which means “great house”

Life during this time wasn’t so bad, workers village (for town planning) discovered at Deir el-Medina provides info

            -had 10 day week (worked 8), given clothing and food by pharaoh, had livestock, literate

Changed the capital from Thebes to Akhetaten (modern Tell el-Amarna)

This break in ties with religion caused Egyptian art to break from tradition

            -created Amarna style

***Egypt is only monotheistic during the Amarna period, named after where Akhenaton moved the capital

            -art during this time heretical

Discussion questions

Ancient Egyptian funerary practices reflect and reveal spiritual beliefs. What were those practices and how were they discovered? Select an object illustrated in this chapter that can be interpreted according to Egyptian beliefs of life and death and explain what it reveals.

One item that nicely illustrates Egyptian beliefs on death is the Judgement of Hunefer before Osiris. This painting shows multiple stages of what happens after death and if the soul is deemed worthy of entering into the afterlife. Other papyri would be placed in tombs that would have paintings and inscriptions of magical words and spells for the afterlife, the compilation of these being called the “Book of the Dead”.

The Judgement of Hunefer begins with Anubis escorting Hunefer to his judgement, which is watched by Ammit, the Eater of the Dead. Hunefer’s heart, a representative of his soul, is weighed against Maat’s feather of truth on the scales of judgement. Upon passing this test, Horus escorts Hunefer to be greeted by Osiris, Nephthys, and Isis along with Osiris’s sons who watch over Hunefer’s organs. At the top, Hunefer after being officially accepted into the afterlife, is kneeling before the gods of the underworld.

This painting reveals that the Egyptians had a very vivid idea of what happened after death, which was dictated by how the individual acted while living.

 

Painted surfaces and the use of color on objects and architectural structures are significant characteristics of Egyptian art. Identify examples of this practice. How do these elements distinguish Egyptian conventions from those of Near Eastern cultures?

Just like how humans are consistently portrayed in Egyptian art, the use of color follows conventions that are also specific and stylized to Egypt. There are six colors that were used in Egyptian art: red, blue, yellow, green, black, and white. Each of these colors held their own significance and symbolism and were used in their own ways. In portrayals of men and women, for example, men were typically colored red while women were colored white/yellow.

An example of where this color scheme has significance is in a papyrus from the Book of the Dead, the Judgement of Hunefer before Osiris. When closely looking at the painting, colors are used specifically to distinguish between dead and alive, human and non-human, then architecture. Painting in the Near East was different from Egyptian in that it was much more randomly done, the colors had no significance themselves and were not employed also to tell a story. In the Near East, color use was much more for decoration and extravagance than storytelling.
