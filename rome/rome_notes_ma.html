Rome

 

Monarchy and Republic (753 – 27 BCE):

Rome was ruled by Etruscan kings until the revolt against Tarquinius Superbus, ending the Roman monarchy

            -the Republic lasted from the revolt against Superbus to the renaming of Augustus to Octavian

The Republic established a new government which gave power mostly to the senate

211 BCE was a significant year because Marcellus, who had conquered a Greek city, brought back art alongside the rest of the usual loot

 

Temple of Portunus

example of a manubial temple  symbolic of military victory and conquest

 

Etruscan characteristics: freestanding columns only around porch, (pseudo-peripteral) high podium, front only steps

Greek characteristics: Ionic columns w/ Ionic frieze, length to width ratio, stone overlaid w/ stucco to imitate marble

The mix of Etruscan and Greek elements makes this distinctly Roman

Early Empire ft. Augustus, Julio-Claudians, Flavians (27 BCE – 96 CE):

Began with the rule of Augustus and his successors into the next period with the Julio-Claudians and Flavians

            -this is the period following the assassination of Caesar

Peace established by Augustus lasted for two centuries and was known as Pax Romana

 

Models for many of the portraits of Augustus were Classical Greek statues

            -wanted to put Rome on the same ground as Athens during its height

 

 

 

 

 

 

Ara Pacis Augustae (Altar of the Augustan Peace)

This monument celebrates Augustus’s establishment of peace

            -reliefs depict mythological subjects and symbolism in the classical style

Ara Pacis is part temple, part propaganda, and part victory monument

Imagery on the top is figural while imagery on the bottom, separated by a meander, is natural

            -natural imagery on bottom includes various wildlife to show fertility and abundance

 

 

Female personification, panel on east façade Ara Pacis

Discussion questions

How is the concept of “verism” reflected in multiple types of Roman art? How do these examples compare to examples of Greek art?

Verism was in some ways totally opposite of Greek art, but in others this style would fit right in. Verism was the act of depicting someone in their most real and gritty form, forgoing the Classical idealism that dominated Greek art for a long time. It does fit in with the Greek Hellenistic style, as that is when emotions and grittiness dominated art and made the Hellenistic era famous.

The Romans used this veristic style a bit differently than the Greeks though, and used it as a way to show the hard work a man has put in and how that backs his character. In Greece, a veristic statue would not be seen as a piece of propaganda but simply for what it was. This was mainly because the Romans viewed the head and body differently when it came to portraiture, so the face was used to convey a lot more in Rome than Greece.

 

How did techniques, styles, and conventions of Roman portraiture change over time? Why was portraiture of elders, statesmen, and others important to Roman society?

Roman portraiture style seemed to change as quickly as the emperors they portrayed. These portraits were used to make political statements and serve as artistic propaganda for the emperor. The emperor with the most portraits of himself, Hadrian, was one that stuck to the Classical Greek style of portraiture and portrayal of the self. This was due to his extreme love for Greece and anything to do with it, so he chose to model himself with the greatness of the past.

Augustus’ portraiture comes with another change, as he is faced with Rome in the aftermath of the assassination of Caesar. His portraiture combined the veristic style with the youthful style to portray multiple character traits, which was the most important thing in Roman portraiture. The combination of old and young features bridges the past with the future, as stylizing himself after previous emperors in the veristic style but also men such as Alexander the Great with youthfulness, Augustus is putting himself on the same level as great leaders.
