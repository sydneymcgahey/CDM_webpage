Napoleon vs Constantine: A Comparison of Triumphal Arches

 

            Triumphal arches are nothing new when it comes to dedicational architecture, as they started to pop up in popularity during the Roman empire. These arches were often built to honor a person or event and normally were architecturally isolated, but still accessible via a roadway to allow for processions. Though these pieces of architecture were popular during the Roman period, only three remain standing today: the Arch of Titus, Arch of Septimius Severus, and Arch of Constantine, the last of which will be compared to the Arc de Triomphe, a famous French historical landmark (Triumphal).

            The Arch of Constantine - Arcus Constantini -, which was commissioned by the Roman Senate in 315 AD at the decennalia - tenth anniversary - of Constantine’s reign, served to commemorate the victory of the emperor against Maxentius in the Battle of the Milvian Bridge in 312 AD. This battle was significant as the Romans saw it as a fight for control of their empire in the west (Arch)sol. The Arch, standing at 20 meters high and 25 meters wide, sits along the Via Triumphalis, which was and still is a heavily trafficked area within Rome. This area, known as the Circus Maximus, also houses the Roman Forum and Flavian Amphitheater, most commonly known as the Colosseum. This piece of architecture, originally created to commemorate and honor, also serves as a piece of propaganda for Constantine. It is done through the scenes it displays and use of spolia from previous emperor’s creations, including Hadrian, Trajan, and Marcus Aurelius (» Arch).

            The second triumphal arch to be discussed, the Arc de Triomphe (Arc de Triomphe de l’Etoile), was commissioned by Napoleon in 1806 after his victory in the Battle of Austerlitz, though it also generally celebrates the victory of French armies. The design of the arch was modeled after neoclassical pieces, notably Roman, by Jean-François-Thérèse Chalgrin. Napoleon sought to make his triumphal arch greater and better, which resulted in a building that is 50 meters tall and 45 meters wide, almost double the size of the Arch of Constantine. This iconic symbol of French identity took thirty years to build, with its completion in 1833 and inauguration in 1836 by the French king, Louis-Philippe (Triumphal). Like the previous Roman arches, the Arc was built along a heavily trafficked roadway, giving it access to processions. Many processions have passed along the Arc, such as Germany in 1871 and 1940 and France with their allies in 1918, 1944, and 1945. The area that the Arc sits on within the roadway allows it to be seen from all twelve avenues that stem off that main area, forming a star-like formation (etoile) and giving the Arc its full name: Arch of Triumph of the Star (Triumphal)bas.

            Triumphal architecture in general has some common elements that define it. The basic form consists of two columns connected by an arch and then crowned by a superstructure, or attica, singular: attic. The attic is commonly where inscriptions would be carved, detailing the exploits and rights of the leader. Traditionally, early attica depicted the victor and his triumphal procession, while later attica depicted solely the emperor. Another common feature of a triumphal arch is the presence of a cornice, often there is more than one cornice on an arch, typically one being on the arch and one on the attic (Triumphal).

            Both the Arc de Triomphe and Arch of Constantine were deliberately placed in their respective locations, though for different reasons that don’t include being along the way of a procession. The placement of these arches is done to create an experience for the viewers when viewing the arches at certain times. The Arch, while situated near the Flavian Amphitheater, does not rely on that to create a visual experience but rather a statue that is no longer there: the Colossus of Sol. The Colossus of Sol, a colossal bronze statue of the sun god, Sol, would have been centered to have been viewed directly from the central arch. The presence of the god Sol is a call to the more familiar god to the Romans, Apollo. Both Augustus and Nero were said to have been under the tutelage of Apollo, so Constantine’s choice of framing the Arch with this sculpture is not out of character (Marlowe). Just like the spolia that was used from previous emperors to partially construct the Arch, Constantine finds a way to use previous great emperors to legitimize his rule.

            The Arc utilizes the idea of framing slightly differently and was constructed to be in line with the setting sun during specific times of the year. It was built directly along the Historical Axis of Paris, which is set at a 26° angle and includes other famous French landmarks. Since being built on this particular axis, around May 10th and August 1st every year, the sun sets along the Historical Axis and therefore directly in the center of the Arc. Around February 7th and November 4th is when the rising sun in the east sets directly under the Arc. Though not having a direct relationship with any sort of sun god, the Arc can still be compared to the Arch in that they both frame light and the idea invincibility and eternity.

            Inscriptions, another important element of triumphal arches, are prevalent on both the Arc de Triomphe and Arch of Constantine. Though both arches have different subject matter in their inscriptions, they equally serve the same purpose of commemoration of military victory and legitimization of leaders. The Arch of Constantine is more classic in its subject matter, detailing the triumphs of the emperor Constantine. The inscription, on the central part of the attic, states: “To the Emperor Caesar Flavius Constantinus, the Greatest, pious, fortunate, the Senate and the people of Rome, by inspiration of divinity and his own great mind with his righteous arms on both the tyrant and his faction in one instant in rightful battle he avenged the republic, dedicated this arch as a memorial to his military victory.” Typical of Roman architecture, this engraving mentions only the emperor and describes his rule as a divine and achieved right, especially after beating a ‘tyrant’. This tyrant in question is Maxentius, who Constantine fought for control over the Roman empire in the west. The Romans viewed this as a battle against barbarism and their victory became more meaningful as it had worked to save their society and culture (» Arch).

            Napoleon takes a more modern route in his choice of inscriptions, and decides to include the names of 660 soldiers, most of whom were high-ranking generals during the Napoleonic Wars and French Revolution. Along with the names, several battle victories from those times in French history are mentioned as well (Pierre). Though these inscriptions do not mention Napoleon or his leadership directly, viewers are still aware of his involvement as they are typically French citizens and are familiar enough with their own history to have the visual literacy to realize this is a monument to French soldiers and most importantly, Napoleon. With the recall to himself through a specific choice of inscription, Napoleon is taking a contemporary approach to naming his achievements. This choice of indirect naming serves as an act of legitimization and possibly even propaganda as viewers are reminded of the greatness of Napoleon and his military achievements for the French people.

            There are some overlaps in slight details pertaining to the buildings that show how past pieces can continue to inspire the present in the smallest of ways. Both arches exhibit similar egg-and-dart molding on the first cornice, right below the attic. This type of molding is common and is also found on the Erectheion on the Acropolis in Athens, Greece. The Erectheion was built long before either arch, again creating a link between motifs and empires. Below the first cornice on the Arc de Triomphe is yet another tie to ancient architecture, as there is a row is the Greek Key motif. This is the most common motif in Greek architecture and is a direct comparison to past great cultures. The Arc and Arch also both feature winged figures in the spandrels, these figures are carrying victory trophies. Both figures are female as well, which can be a call to the goddess of victory, Nike.

The reliefs and designs found on both arches are also important in the message that is intended for viewers and display similar themes to each other. They depict scenes that relate to military conquest, though the Arch of Constantine is older fashioned in that it is specifically about Constantine, whereas the Arc de Triomphe has metaphorical reliefs and does not depict Napoleon directly. Scenes found depicted in reliefs on the attic of the Arch of Constantine include Constantine’s profectio “departure” from Milan, siege of Verona, Battle of the Milvian Bridge, Constantine’s adventus “arrival” into Rome, his adlocutio “speech” to the Roman people, and liberalitas “ritual purification via sacrifice”. This set of scenes found in the reliefs are seen in order when the viewer walks around the Arch in a clockwise fashion, starting from the south side. On the west side of the Arch, which is the beginning of Constantine’s conquest is a moon engraving while on the east side of the Arch, at the victorious end of the conquest, is the sun. This is directly symbolic of the state of the Roman empire and Constantine’s role in it, the moon being the dark period of war and rising sun when Constantine comes back victories after defeating the tyrant (Elsner).

All of the panels featured on the attic show an activity associated with leadership, such as hunting, public speaking, and offering sacrifices. The adlocutio and liberalitas are both pieces of spolia from Marcus Aurelius, thought to have been taken from a previous triumphal arch. The heads of the emperor in these pieces were later re-carved to depict Constantine (Antonine). This act, known as damnatio memoriae, is often a derogatory action and is done to condemn someone, however, Constantine took this as an opportunity to turn it around and use this combination of techniques as a way to use previous leaders to legitimize himself. This method is also used in another piece of spolia, this one from Hadrian.

One of the Hadrianic roundels depicts a boar-hunting scene, which has been popular in leadership art pieces since the Neo-Assyrians. The row of recycled Hadrianic pieces are surrounded by porphyry, which is purple in color and commonly symbolic of higher class. Once again, Constantine had decided to re-carve the head of the emperor, Hadrian, into his own likeness. Like typical triumphal arch architecture, the Arch of Constantine features columns of the Corinthian order that frame each smaller arch. These columns are topped by four Dacian captives, which is a piece of spolia from Trajan. The extensive use of spolia by Constantine in his commemorative architecture exhibits an urge to use the past to reinforce the present, and therefore legitimize Constantine as emperor.

Though the Arc de Triomphe does not go to the extent of spolia use that the Arch of Constantine does, the relief work on the Arc is still notable. The largest and most popular reliefs are on each of the four bases comprising the Arc: “The Triumph” by Cortot, “Resistance” by Antoine Étex, “Peace” also by Antoine Étex, and “The Departure of the Volunteers” or popularly known as “La Marseillaise” by François Rude. These four reliefs depict events from the Revolution and First Empire. “The Triumph” features characteristics from Greek art and society such as the presence of Nike and laurel wreaths. This relief shows only one figure nude, who more specifically is a woman who has her genitals covered but chest is bare. Based on traditional Greek depictions of nude women in art, this woman must be the goddess Aphrodite.

“Resistance” features a more emotionally evoking image as viewers are faced with struggling civilians who seem to be begging a hero for help, based on the heroic nudity and drawn weapon of the central male figure. Heroic nudity was a popular characteristic among the Hellenistic period and is an obvious call to ancient Greece. “Peace”, another relief done by Étex, features mostly all of the same figures from the previous relief, “Resistance.” The difference is between the divine figure, “Peace” features someone who looks quite similar to Athena per her aegis, staff, and plumed helmet. The worried civilians begging the sword-drawn warrior are now appeased, the warrior next to them with his weapon sheathed and now standing in a calm, contrapposto stance.

The final and most popular of the reliefs on the base of the Arc is “The Departure of the Volunteers”, also known as “La Marseillaise”. This relief depicts the same Athena-looking figure and soldiers, most likely French, preparing to head into battle as they all have their weapons drawn and are posed seemingly ready to strike. One figure in particular is in full heroic nudity, featuring the muscular build and small genitals.  

Above the four base reliefs are bas-reliefs - reliefs that project very little from the background - that span around the entire Arc. These bas-reliefs depict events from the Revolution and Napoleonic Era and were carved by various French artists: “La Bataille d’Aboukir” by Seurre the Elder, “Les Funérailles du General Marceau” by P.H. Lamaire, “La Bataille de Jemappes” by Carlo Marochetti, “La Prise d’Alexandrie” by J.E. Chaponnière, “Le Passage du Pont d’Arcole” by FJ.J. Feuchère, “La Bataille d’Austerlitz” by J.F.T. Gechter. Above these bas-reliefs is a frieze that depicts soldiers leaving for and returning to battle. Moving further up the Arc, above the frieze are more dedications to Revolutionary and Napoleonic War victories (Pierre). Top of Form

The Arch of Constantine, constructed to commemorate the victory of the Battle of the Milvian Bridge, serves as a piece of political propaganda, made with spolia from the creations of past great emperors like Hadrian, Trajan, and Marcus Aurelius. Constantine skillfully employs these elements to not only commemorate his triumph but also to legitimize his rule by associating himself with the greatness of previous Roman leaders.

On the other hand, the Arc de Triomphe reflects Napoleon's ambition for grandeur as it towers over the Arch of Constantine in both size and scale. The inscriptions on the Arc provide a modern twist to the traditional triumphal arch inscription subject matter while commemorating French soldiers and indirectly legitimizing Napoleon's leadership and serving as a form of propaganda.

The reliefs on these arches further aid the leaders in their intentions to legitimize. The Arch of Constantine, with its scenes of Constantine's conquests and spolia from previous emperors, showcases a fusion of classical elements. Meanwhile, the Arc de Triomphe employs reliefs depicting events from the Revolution and First Empire, reflecting a more metaphorical and collective representation of French identity.
